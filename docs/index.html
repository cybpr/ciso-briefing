📋 Executive Summary

The week of May 5–11, 2026 represents one of the most consequential seven-day threat windows of the year. Five high-to-critical severity items demand immediate leadership attention across three distinct attack surfaces: network perimeter infrastructure, Linux server estates, and enterprise SaaS/cloud platforms.

The most urgent threat is CVE-2026-0300, an unpatched, actively exploited root-level RCE in Palo Alto PAN-OS firewalls — the very devices organizations rely on for perimeter defense. Simultaneously, the Instructure Canvas breach — now confirmed as the largest educational data breach in history — exposes 275 million individuals across nearly 9,000 institutions and carries material downstream phishing and credential-stuffing risk for any enterprise with affiliated academic users. Two consecutive Linux kernel LPE chains (Copy Fail and Dirty Frag) are being actively exploited in cloud and containerized environments, while the worm-capable PCPJack credential-theft framework is systematically harvesting API keys, SSH credentials, and cloud secrets from misconfigured Docker/Kubernetes deployments. The ShinyHunters threat group is prominently behind multiple high-profile incidents this week, demonstrating both breadth and operational tempo.

Some cybercriminal groups can break into networks and begin spreading laterally in under 30 seconds, and AI-assisted attacks are rising sharply — zero-day vulnerabilities are being exploited faster than security teams can respond. The window between disclosure and exploitation has collapsed; response capabilities must keep pace.

PAN-OS Captive Portal Zero-Day: Unauthenticated Root RCE on Perimeter Firewalls

RPS: 9.14  |  S=9.3×0.45 + E=9×0.35 + X=9×0.20 🔗 security.paloaltonetworks.com/CVE-2026-0300
MITRE ATT&CK: T1190 (Exploit Public-Facing Application)  |  T1068 (Privilege Escalation)  |  T1033 (System Owner Discovery)  |  T1003 (Credential Dumping)  |  T1562.001 (Impair Defenses: Disable or Modify Tools)

CVE-2026-0300 is a case of unauthenticated remote code execution carrying a CVSS score of 9.3 when the User-ID Authentication Portal is configured to enable access from the internet or any untrusted network. A buffer overflow in the Captive Portal service allows an unauthenticated attacker to execute arbitrary code with root privileges on PA-Series and VM-Series firewalls by sending specially crafted packets. Starting April 9, 2026, there were unsuccessful exploitation attempts against a PAN-OS device; a week later, attackers successfully achieved RCE and injected shellcode, then immediately conducted log cleanup by clearing crash kernel messages and removing crash core dump files, before deploying tools with root privileges and conducting Active Directory enumeration using the firewall's service account credentials. Unit 42 has attributed the observed activity to CL-STA-1132, a threat cluster likely sponsored by a nation-state, whose objective appears to be Active Directory enumeration and deployment of open-source tunneling tools after gaining root access. This represents one of the worst possible scenarios of 2026: a critical, unauthenticated RCE flaw in a perimeter firewall with confirmed active exploitation, no patch available, and a federal mitigation deadline already in effect — with Shadowserver identifying more than 5,800 PAN-OS VM-Series firewalls exposed to the public internet.

The hack is considered the largest educational security breach on record as of May 8, 2026, affecting 8,809 universities, educational ministries, and other institutions worldwide, with particularly significant implications in the United States where Canvas is used by 41% of higher education institutions. The hacking group ShinyHunters claimed to have stolen 3.65 terabytes of data (approximately 275 million records), including private messages exchanged between students and teachers. ShinyHunters has set a ransom deadline of end of day May 12, 2026, threatening to leak all data if Instructure does not make contact. Days after the first attack, ShinyHunters is applying pressure with ransom messages on school login portals. For enterprises, the primary risk is the downstream availability of 275 million verified email addresses and institutional credentials that will fuel targeted spear-phishing and credential-stuffing campaigns against corporate accounts shared with affiliated educational institutions.

🔴 ACT NOW CVE-2026-43284 / CVE-2026-43500  |  Unpatched (partial)
The Hacker News / Tenable / Microsoft  |  May 7–9, 2026

Linux "Dirty Frag": Second Universal Kernel LPE in 8 Days — Public PoC, Partial Patches, Container Escape Risk

RPS: 8.65  |  S=9×0.45 + E=8×0.35 + X=8×0.20 🔗 tenable.com — Dirty Frag FAQ
MITRE ATT&CK: T1068 (Exploitation for Privilege Escalation)  |  T1611 (Escape to Host)  |  T1059.004 (Unix Shell)  |  T1014 (Rootkit)  |  T1210 (Exploitation of Remote Services — post-LPE lateral movement)

Dirty Frag is a new, local privilege escalation (LPE) vulnerability impacting the Linux kernel, described as a successor to Copy Fail (CVE-2026-31431, CVSS 7.8), a recently disclosed LPE flaw that has already come under active exploitation in the wild. CVE-2026-43284 and CVE-2026-43500 were disclosed by researcher Hyunwoo Kim on May 8, 2026, after another party broke a coordinated embargo; the pair of vulnerabilities allows an unprivileged local user to corrupt arbitrary page caches and pivot to root on many Linux distributions, with a working proof of concept published the same day before any distribution shipped patched kernels. The researcher notes that Dirty Frag can be triggered regardless of whether the algif_aead module is available — meaning systems where the Copy Fail mitigation has already been applied remain vulnerable to Dirty Frag. Dirty Frag enables escalation through vulnerable kernel networking components including esp4, esp6, and rxrpc, with public reporting indicating the exploit is designed to provide more reliable privilege escalation than traditional race-condition techniques; it may be leveraged after initial compromise through SSH access, web-shell execution, or container escape. With a working public PoC released ahead of patches and exploitation reduced to a handful of standard syscalls, defenders should assume any local foothold on an unpatched host can become root within seconds.

Linux "Copy Fail" (CVE-2026-31431): Actively Exploited Kernel LPE Targets Cloud, Kubernetes, and CI/CD Environments

RPS: 8.21  |  S=8×0.45 + E=8×0.35 + X=8×0.20 🔗 Microsoft Security Blog — Copy Fail
MITRE ATT&CK: T1068 (Exploitation for Privilege Escalation)  |  T1611 (Escape to Host)  |  T1543 (Create or Modify System Process)  |  T1055 (Process Injection)

Microsoft Defender is investigating this high-severity local privilege escalation vulnerability affecting multiple major Linux distributions including Red Hat, SUSE, Ubuntu, and AWS Linux — with a working exploit already in the wild, organizations should act quickly to detect, mitigate, and reduce risk. What makes this flaw particularly alarming is its exploitability: a 732-byte Python script is all an unprivileged local user needs to reliably escalate privileges to root. Critically, exploitation requires no root privileges inside containers, no kernel modules, and no network access, making it a powerful post-exploitation tool in containerized environments including Kubernetes clusters and Docker CI runners. Successful exploitation leads to full root privilege escalation with high impact to confidentiality, integrity, and availability, and could facilitate container breakout, multi-tenant compromise, and lateral movement within shared environments — its reliability, stealth (in-memory-only modification), and cross-platform applicability make it particularly dangerous in cloud, CI/CD, and Kubernetes environments. CISA added CVE-2026-31431 to its KEV catalog on May 1, 2026, with a mandatory remediation deadline of May 15, 2026 for all federal civilian agencies.

SentinelLABS identified PCPJack, a credential theft framework that worms across exposed cloud infrastructure and removes artifacts associated with TeamPCP, a threat actor persona who claimed several high-profile supply chain intrusions throughout early 2026; the toolset harvests credentials from cloud, container, developer, productivity, and financial services, then exfiltrates the data through attacker-controlled infrastructure while attempting to spread to additional hosts. The main orchestrator conducts local credential theft, propagates the toolset to other hosts by exploiting five known CVEs (CVE-2025-55182, CVE-2025-29927, CVE-2026-1357, CVE-2025-9501, and CVE-2025-48703), and uses Telegram for command-and-control. The malware also scans Instance Metadata Service (IMDS) endpoints, Kubernetes service accounts, and Docker instances for credentials associated with Anthropic, Digital Ocean, Discord, Google API, Grafana Cloud, HashiCorp Vault, OnePassword, and OpenAI, transmitting them to an external server. Unlike typical cloud-focused malware, PCPJack does not deploy cryptominers; the services it targets suggest monetization through credential theft, fraud, spam, extortion, or resale of stolen access. The "PCP replaced" C2 telemetry field — tracking competitive evictions from compromised hosts — indicates a highly organized, operationally-competitive threat actor operating at scale.
