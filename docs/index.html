The vulnerability, tracked as CVE-2026-0300, has been described as a case of unauthenticated remote code execution, carrying a CVSS score of 9.3 when the User-ID Authentication Portal is configured to enable access from the internet or any untrusted network. Starting April 9, 2026, there were unsuccessful exploitation attempts against a PAN-OS device; a week later, the attackers successfully achieved RCE and injected shellcode, immediately conducting log cleanup by clearing crash kernel messages, deleting nginx crash entries, and removing crash core dump files, followed four days later by deployment of tools with root privileges and Active Directory enumeration using the firewall's service account credentials. 🔴 ACT NOW CVE-2026-31431 "Copy Fail" — Linux Kernel LPE Affecting Every Distro Since 2017
RPS: 9.0
Source Theori / Xint (CERT-EU, CISA KEV, Help Net Security) Published Apr 29 – May 04, 2026
MITRE ATT&CK T1068 (Exploitation for Privilege Escalation)  |  T1611 (Escape to Host)  |  T1548 (Abuse Elevation Control Mechanism)  |  T1014 (Rootkit)
Score Inputs Severity: 9  |  Exploitability: 9  |  Exposure: 9   →   RPS = (9×0.45)+(9×0.35)+(9×0.20) = 9.0
A logic flaw in the kernel's crypto subsystem — in authencesn chained through AF_ALG and splice() — lets any unprivileged local user escalate to root with a 732-byte exploit that works unmodified across every mainstream distribution built since 2017. Unlike the Dirty Cow and Dirty Pipe Linux kernel LPE vulnerabilities, Copy Fail can be exploited without having to win a race condition, and the same exploit will work on many systems. Successful exploitation leads to full root privilege escalation and could facilitate container breakout, multi-tenant compromise, and lateral movement within shared environments; its reliability, stealth (in-memory-only modification), and cross-platform applicability make it particularly dangerous in cloud, CI/CD, and Kubernetes environments. CISA added Copy Fail to its Known Exploited Vulnerabilities catalog and ordered US federal civilian agencies to address it by May 15, 2026. Patches are available for Debian, Ubuntu, AlmaLinux, SUSE, Fedora, and CloudLinux; RHEL users are still awaiting vendor patches. Interim mitigation: blacklist the algif_aead kernel module via grub initcall until vendor patches are applied.

The Iranian state-sponsored hacking group known as MuddyWater has been attributed to a ransomware attack described as a "false flag" operation; the attack was observed by Rapid7 in early 2026, leveraging social engineering via Microsoft Teams, and although the incident initially appeared consistent with a Chaos RaaS group, evidence points to a targeted state-backed attack masquerading as opportunistic extortion. Once contact was established, the threat actor initiated interactive screen-sharing sessions, executing discovery commands including ipconfig /all, whoami, and net start; victims were explicitly instructed to type their credentials into locally created text files named credentials.txt and to add attacker-controlled devices to their MFA configurations. Throughout the intrusion, the attackers never deployed file-encrypting ransomware, suggesting Chaos artifacts were planted as false flags to hide state-sponsored activity; the inclusion of extortion elements served to focus defensive efforts on immediate impact, likely delaying identification of underlying persistence mechanisms established via remote access tools such as DWAgent or AnyDesk. These findings underscore the growing convergence of state-sponsored intrusion activity and cybercriminal tradecraft to obscure attribution, with the use of a RaaS framework enabling the actor to blur distinctions between state-sponsored and financially motivated cybercrime.
